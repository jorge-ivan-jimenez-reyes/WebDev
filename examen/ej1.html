Another useful distinction to be drawn here is between the Internet as a source of data and the Internet as a social field of study. Internet research is concerned not only with the study of on-line behavior (what people do in virtual and mediated environments), but is also concerned with using computer-based tools and computer-accessible populations to study human behavior in general. On the one hand, then, Internet researchers can access if not potentially infinite data, at least a larger amount of data than is possible in the physical world.

Although Internet technology is changing fast, the focus of computer-mediated communications has been primarily on text-based virtual realities, because a majority of studies reflect the fact that only text-based technology is available on-line. Such a technological limitation, rather than being a communication handicap, is an imagination-enhancing challenge. It seems reasonable to suppose that in certain situations, people will prefer textual interactions, even when more advanced technology becomes widely available in the future.

Internet measurement does raise some ethical issues. A pressing problem in Internet research is privacy; researching as an unobtrusive observer and collecting data, for example, inevitably poses a major ethical problem. Neither can this problem be avoided ad hoc, by using laboratory experiments, because those involve a small number of consenting people.
